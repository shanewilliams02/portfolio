Neighbor
Redesign

Role: UX Designer
Duration: 12 Weeks

Introduction

HelloNeighbor is an in-home elder care startup created to help customers find, schedule, and purchase in-home care for themselves or their loved ones online.

I was brought on to redesign HelloNeighbor’s landing page and design a prototype of the company’s booking interface.

Problem

The question I was brought on to answer was, I was brought on to answer was, how can we design a simple, straight-forward solution that can be used by older, technologically literate, but not necessarily tech-savvy users to solve a complex challenge tied to even more complex emotions?

Solution

Because booking in-home care for loved ones is relatively rare and research done throughout the project showed that HelloNeighbor’s potential customers tended to be less tech-savvy and older users, I updated the presentation to focus on usability, accessibility, transparency, and trust.

The landing page was designed to quickly and easily establish exactly how HelloNeighbor works while using branding elements to convey a sense of trust and professionalism while at the same time being open and welcoming.

I designed the HelloNeighbor interface to let users quickly schedule, customize, purchase, and review care sessions. My primary goal was to alleviate as much stress and complexity from the booking process as possible. I worked to empower users and encourage engagement in all stages of the care process. Users can monitor the care they or their loved ones receive and provide feedback on HelloNeighbor care providers.

Background

Before I was brought on, the team at HelloNeighbor had already done some market research, lo-fidelity prototyping, and created a single-page landing page site.

I obviously wanted to honor the team's work. However, I knew that to understand the problem and work toward a potential solution, I’d need to develop a thorough understanding of the in-home care space, the competitive landscape, and most importantly, the challenges faced by both those seeking and providing care.

Stakeholder Interviews

I started my research by conducting a series of interviews with HelloNeighbor’s principal stakeholders to get a firm grasp on their vision for the company, their current understanding of the market, and their work before bringing me on. I spoke with HelloNeighbor’s CEO, one of their investors, and the front-end developer who designed the original HelloNeighbor landing page.

Based on how the service works, the HelloNeighbor team knew that there would be two primary sets of users on the platform: those seeking care and HelloNeighbor-employed care providers. They also understood the critical role design would play in building and reinforcing trust.

They had a picture of their users as older and less tech-savvy. I needed to perform research to either confirm or debunk that assumption. The team was also unaware of the other players were in the market. So that’s where I turned next.

Competitive Analysis

To gain a thorough understanding of the in-home care space, I used Jaime Levy’s Competitive Analysis Matrix to audit the marketing sites and web applications of three major direct competitors: Honor, HomeTeamCare, and Carelinx. I was particularly interested in how these sites conveyed their value proposition, their onboarding processes, as well as how they conveyed professionalism and established trust through design.

Then, I created a SWOT analysis of these companies to identify what opportunities HelloNeighbor could take advantage of to establish itself in the marketplace.

I also curated a list of companies with track records of success in designing for our most vulnerable moments to serve as design inspiration, including Airbnb, Uber, and Lyft.

User Surveys

To better understand the needs and challenges of care seekers and care providers I designed surveys to identify how people were already providing or finding care for their loved ones and how they coordinated care with their family members. I also wanted an overview of potential users’ technological behavior and their digital wardrobe.

From professional care providers, I was eager to find out how they were matched with clients, any challenges they encountered in their work, and what methods and tools they currently used to keep track of their various clients and appointments.

Key Survey Findings

Business Goals / Challenges

  • Only 5% of participants had used online resources to find care in the past. Word-of-mouth referral was the most common form of finding care providers.

  • While the respondents ranged in age, 93% were 40 years old or older.

  • 80% of respondents used their smartphones for online browsing (shopping, banking, services)

Design Goals / Challenges

  • Educating users on how the platform works and how it differs from traditional in-home care models would be critical.

  • Following accessibility and usability best practices would be key to platform success.

  • HelloNeighbor would require a responsive platform.

User Interviews and Personas

I conducted interviews with several families who were caring for their loved ones as well as some professional care providers. These conversations were invaluable in fleshing out the data and a better understanding of HelloNeighbor’s users and their needs. The interviews real value to the process was in highlighting the variety of challenges people encounter in finding and providing elder care.

I created three personas for each user group from the combined survey and interview data. I used the personas as reminders of the goals and pain points of our potential users throughout the remainder of the process to challenge and validate my design decisions.

Kathy - Head of Household

Goals
  • Easily integrate care appointments with her iCal and share them with her family.
  • Get issues/questions addressed quickly and efficiently regarding her mother's care.
  • See ratings and reviews of caregivers from other customers.
Frustrations
  • Downloading and using new technology especially on an older system.
  • Coordinating her client load across multiple agencies by hand.
  • Spending time learning apps and programs which she doesn't really use.

Sherry - The Veteran

Goals
  • To quickly get in contact with her clients or their families via phone or text.

  • The ability to see and update her schedule of appointments.
  • Able to focus on care and not learning a new app.
Frustrations
  • Having to coordinate appointments manually outside of her iCal.
  • Having to hunt down customer service when she has questions.
  • Spending a lot of time learning a new app when there are other things she'd rather or be doing.

User Stories and Flows

With research at the ready, I drew up a series of user flows for identifying key tasks for care seekers and care providers. For each group, I broke flows out into new and returning users, prioritizing each groups user stories based on what each group would need to achieve their most important goals. Helping users meet their highest priorities was the driving force behind creating the prototype for our Minimal Viable Product.

Then, with the user stories established, I mapped out user flows diagraming the basic journey users would take through HelloNeighbor’s app.

Prototyping: Sketches and Lo-Fi Mockups

Starting with pen and paper, I sketched out numerous variations on designs for the landing page and the booking UI. By sketching first, I could rapidly explore a wide range of design possibilities and get feedback and input before committing to digital tools. Getting feedback early and often was essential.

It was through feedback gathered using sketches that I discovered my initial landing page concepts didn't convey exactly how HelloNeighbor’s platform worked and was able to start working toward a possible solution.

I took the basic design concepts and used Balsamiq to create lo-fidelity mockups which I then used to gather another round of user and stakeholder feedback. From here, I moved into creating hi-fidelity mockups in Sketch and crafted a clickable prototype with InvisionApp for user testing.

Branding

All branding decisions were made to establish HelloNeighbor as trustworthy and professional as well as open and welcoming like, well a neighbor. During the research phase, I’d learned that HelloNeighbor would be catering to a variety of ages, physical and cognitive abilities and levels of tech-savviness.

I prioritized simplicity and readability in typography and a color palette that was both modern and bright but also professional and trustworthy.

Color Palette

With HelloNeighbor’s brand refresh I opted for color, imagery, and typography that would immediately establish the company as friendly yet professional while being modern but familiar.

I moved from the teal and Pantone green of the original styleguide to richer blues and greens often used by healthcare, banks, and social media companies. A safe choice? Sure, but these are the very companies we trust our money, health, data to and I believed HelloNeighbor could leverage those associations.

More importantly, the original brand colors failed to meet accessibility contrast standards by a wide margin. So for the new palette, I worked until I found shades that met AA ( and AAA for large text) contrast standards.

It was a delicate balancing act and took a good deal of tinkering with color pickers and contrast checkers but I was able to settle on a palette that would allow low-vision users to use HelloNeighbor while still conveying the company’s brand values.

Typography

My top priority in choosing a typeface was readability and legibility across devices. I knew I wanted to favor function over form and familiarity over flash. This was a case where if someone was noticing my choices of typeface, I was doing something wrong.

I chose Roboto for body text precisely because of its ease of readability, ubiquity, and lack of distinction. For headings, I opted for fellow sans serif Muli, which has just enough distinctive characteristics to provide enough contrast.

In-person and remote user testing quickly revealed surfaced several key issues. These included a critical issue of requesting billing information during the onboarding and consultation booking flow. User testing also uncovered a number of issues with the design’s overall usability and content.

While requesting credit card info was a seen as a way to ensure the safety of HN staff conducting consultation sessions, users tested were suspicious of having to provide credit card info just to schedule a consultation to discuss care needs. User testing also uncovered a number of issues with the design’s overall usability and content.

A key lesson from this stage was that was that these kind of critical issues could have been revealed sooner in the process by more robust user testing with paper and lo-fi prototypes.

User Testing Findings

Confusion During Onboarding

  • 3/5 participants had issues with the initial onboarding flow of the site particularly with the request for billing info during the onboarding/consultation flow.

    “Why am I being asked for my billing information now? If I saw that I would immediately quit this site.” -Caroline O.

    Copy needs clarity

  • 2/5 were confused about some of the wording used in the branding on the site. Particular about terminology referring to Care Providers as “Neighbors”

    “First it says ‘neighbor’ then it says, ‘Are you a Care Provider?’ It’s a bit confusing. -Knowles A.

Difficulty Locating Sign Out

  • Users unfamiliar with UI patterns had difficulty logging out from the dashboard screen.

    “I don’t know how to sign out…..Oh wow, I never would have caught that.” -Nerissa W.

    Offerings unclear

  • Several users commented that they didn't realize HelloNeighbor was an booking application from the homepage.

    “I get that the company provides care but how do you set it up? -Janine W.

Revisions Based on Testing

Based on the feedback from users, I made a series of changes to the HelloNeighbor prototype to address the the a number of the issues discovered:

  • I revamped the consultation flow eliminating the initial request for users' billing informationDecrease stress on families with elderly loved ones
  • Updated the in-app sidebar navigation to make all functions and options visible
  • Added "We're Transforming In-Home Care" section to the landing page with accompanying imagery to better define HelloNeighbor's value proposition and services
  • Chose higher contrast brand colors to adhere to accessibility standards.
  • Reworked landing page copy regarding Care Providers/Employees for greater clarity

Conclusion and Takeaways

Unfortunately for a variety of reasons out of my control, HelloNeighbor never got off the ground. In spite of the project was incredibly valuable in my development as a designer.

Looking back, there are countless things I'd change about how everything from how I handled certain areas of the design process, to my visual design choices (Easy on the heavy line-work there, kid! Also, remember there are colors besides green and blue).

Lessons Learned

  • Be willing to get creative in finding survey participants. During the research phase, strapped for interview subjects I ended up cold-calling over of in-home care agencies to contact care professionals for interviews
  • Don't fall in love with your solutions, especially early on. Explore as many options you can as early as possible and avoid getting locked into a particular solution. I could have created much better solutions if I'd pushed myself further and been more comfortable iterating rapidly
  • First things first and that means mobile first as new designer I was more comfortable designing for desktop. Also, based on what surveys and interviews told us about potential users' tech usage it seemed like a smart play to hold off on mobile.

    This cost me in terms of time as well as leading me to make certain design decisions that wouldn't fare well responsively
  • Test everything with users including lo-fi sketches via paper prototypes. I could have gathered valuable information feedback earlier in the design process saving time and effort when generating my high-fidelity mockups
